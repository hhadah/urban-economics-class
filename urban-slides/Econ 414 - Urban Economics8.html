Congestion Costs

  • We fix the residential location, and commuters respond to increases in commuting costs by changing either their route to work or choosing a different time of the day

  • The freeway connects the suburb to downtown, and TT is the number of cars on the freeway

  • Speed is a function of the number of commuters using the freeway at the same time

  • When the number of cars reaches the threshold TT, there is congestion, and the speed decreases sharply as T increases beyond TT

Congestion Costs

To derive the connection between commuting costs and TT, assume the existence of a monetary cost mm and a time cost valued at the hourly wage ww.

  • The total commute trip cost is g=m+wDsg=m+wDs, where DD is the distance in miles (freeway length), and ss is the speed per hour. Hence, DsDs is the duration of the trip
  • When the freeway is not congested (number of drivers less than or equal TT), an additional commuter does not increase the commute trip. However, when T>TT>T, those additional drivers slow down everybody else, increasing their time cost wDswDs

Congestion Externality

  • One can write g=g(T)g=g(T) to represent the individual commuting cost - remember ss depends on TT. When the freeway is congested, g(T)g(T) is upward sloping

  • The aggregate commuting cost is Tg(T)Tg(T) (the total number of cars times the cost per car)

  • To see the externality, take the derivative of the aggregate cost with respect to TT:

ddT(aggregate commuting cost)=MC=g(T)Cost to the driver+Tg(T)ExternalityddT(aggregate commuting cost)=MC=g(T)Cost to the driver+Tg(T)Externality

  • The formula shows the effect of one additional car in a congested freeway on the aggregate costs. In other words, the formula shows the marginal cost MCMC of an extra car

  • The aggregate cost increases due to i) the individual cost of the added car and ii) the externality damage imposed by the added car on the others

Private and Social Costs

  • From the formula, we know that MC=g(T)+Tg(T)MC=g(T)+Tg(T). The average cost ACAC is the total cost Tg(T)Tg(T) divided by the number of cars TT. Hence, AC=g(T)AC=g(T)
  • Rewriting the formula:

  • Both MCMC and ACAC depends on TT. When there is no congestion, both curves coincide. However, when there is traffic congestion, the MCMC lies above ACAC and the vertical distance between the curves is equal to the externality damage resulting from an added car

The Demand for Freeway Use

  • To commute from the suburb to downtown, the drivers have three options: train, country road, and freeway

  • For each commuter, there will be a preferred route, i.e., the one that has the lowest cost. On the other hand, the cost of the freeway is the same for everyone g(T)g(T)

  • This idea can be used to derive the individual demands. The cost of the freeway needs to be measured relative to the next best alternative (say country road/train). Then, sort drivers on the basis of their willingness to pay to use the freeway (in other words, the alternate cost).

As you add more and more commuters, there you have the demand curve

The Equilibrium

  • In equilibrium, commuters 1 through TeqTeq use the freeway, while commuters Teq+1Teq+1 through n use alternate routes

  • Equilibrium means no driver has an incentive to switch routes. In other words, no commuter would be better off switching to his alternate route, and no alternate-route user would be better off switching to the freeway

  • Commuter TeqTeq is indifferent between the freeway and his alternate route. What about commuters kk and nn?

The Social Optimum

  • The commuters do not take into account the externality damage they impose to other drivers

    • The socially optimal traffic allocation minimizes the total cost of commuting. In other words, the socially optimal allocation is one in which total commuting costs cannot be reduced by switching any commuter between routes

  • Now, commuters 1 through ToptTopt use the freeway, while commuters Topt+1Topt+1 through n use alternate routes

  • Commuters Topt+1Topt+1 throgh TeqTeq switch from the freeway to their alternate routes

Optimal Freeway Capacity

  • When the demand curve intersects the MCMC curve, the total commuting cost is minimized, and the optimal number of drivers is ToptTopt

  • As you can see, the optimal TT is greater than TT, and there will be congestion. Therefore, forcing the TT to be equal or lower than TT is not in society's interest

  • What happens when they increase the capacity of the freeway? The curves slide over to the right, reaching a higher threshold TT

Congestion Pricing

Congestion Tolls

  • The market equilibrium has too many freeway commuters since no individual has an incentive to consider the social cost of using the freeway

  • The consumers only look at private costs ACAC in making decisions, and to get the optimal amount of drivers using the freeway, one needs to get the prices right

  • The optimal outcome can be achieved by charging congestion tolls: this will raise commuter's private cost ACAC until it coincides with the MCMC curve, internalizing the externality

To generate this coincidence, the toll charged needs to be equal to the height ee, i.e., the vertical distance between ACAC and MCMC at the optimal point ToptTopt

The London Congestion charge

  • Concerns about London's traffic congestion in the 1990s led the Department of Transport to start research about the effects of a potential congestion charge

  • The first study proposed a 4.00 toll on each trip entering Central London

  • The 2000 report focused instead on an "area license": a day pass that allows vehicles to travel into and around the zone as frequently as desired for a fixed charge

  • On February 17, 2003, London imposed a 5.00 daily charge for driving or parking a vehicle on public roads within the congestion charge zone between the hours of 7:00 a.m. to 6:30 p.m., Monday to Friday

  • The charge was based on extensive modeling of household behavior and the resulting traffic patterns instead of a measure of the marginal cost of congestion. Other scenarios were evaluated, with charges at 2.50 and 10.00

  • That charge was assumed to be accompanied by both reduced congestion and improvements in public transportation

Congestion charge boundaries

Costs and Benefits

  • The charge reduced traffic by 15%

  • The time spent in traffic jams dropped sharply - around 30%

  • Traffic level in the bordering areas fell in almost all areas after the scheme's second year. Besides, there was also an increase in the journey time reliability

  • One of the congestion charges' goals was to reallocate road space from private cars to public transportation, and the number of individuals entering central London by bus rose by 38%.

Source: Jonathan Leape, JEP 2006

Costs and Benefits

  • The total estimated annual costs of the congestion charging scheme are 163 million, while the total annual benefits are 230 million

  • The costs are a substantial fraction of the benefits

    • These high costs have raised questions about the operational efficiency of the London scheme. That also suggests the need for careful analysis before extrapolating the benefits of the congestion pricing to other cities

  • Overall, the London congestion charge has been a success
