Housing Demand and Tenure Choice

Marcelino Guerra

March 23, 2020

Introduction


  • So far, we have treated housing as a mere consumption of floor space. In reality, housing is a bundle of attributes - number of bedrooms/bathrooms, house condition, neighborhood characteristics (crime, poverty, schools, public transportation), etc.

  • The fact that housing is also an investment was suppressed, as well as other elements that affect the cost of housing such as mortgage, taxes, depreciation, among others

  • The goal of this lecture is to provide a fuller picture of housing. To do that, we introduce the hedonic model, where the relationship between housing prices and housing attributes is estimated

  • Also, we drop the assumption that everyone is a renter and take tenure choice decision seriously, measuring costs and benefits of owning and renting a house

Hedonic Model of Housing Demand

Hedonic Model


  • Instead of treating housing consumption using a single variable "floor space", we assume that consumers have demands for individual dwelling attributes

  • To isolate these individual demands empirically, the first step is to estimate a function that relates the actual selling price of the house to the property's characteristics

  • Estimating the equation below with real data, one gets the vanilla version of hedonic regression. The estimated coefficients ^1^1, ^2^2 , ^3^3, ..., are known as "implicit prices" of the housing attributes

Sale pricei=0+1Bedroomsi+2Bathroomsi+3Lot sizei+...+iSale pricei=0+1Bedroomsi+2Bathroomsi+3Lot sizei+...+i

  • Those values represent, for example, the effect on the sale price of increasing the lot size while holding the other home features fixed

Costs of Housing - Preliminaries

  • Assume again that housing is measured in a unidimensional fashion

  • When housing is rented, the rental price per unit is pp

  • When a consumer owns the house she lives, there are mortgage payment, property taxes, depreciation, capital gains, maintenance costs, among others

  • To capture these costs, denote

    • V=vqV=vq the value of the house, where vv is the purchase price per unit, and qq the amount of housing consumption
    • ii the mortgage interest rate
    • hh the property tax rate
    • dd the depreciation rate
    • gg the rate of capital gains

  • Assume the mortgage loan equals house's full value, and ignore costs of maintenance and isurance. The total cost of the owner-occupier is given by (i+h+dg)V=(i+h+dg)vq(i+h+dg)V=(i+h+dg)vq. The cost per unit (when q=1q=1) is (i+h+dg)v(i+h+dg)v

Tax Treatment of Housing

  • Depreciation isn't tax deductible for owner-occupier

  • Neither of the housing benefits is taxed for owner-occupiers

  • The gain to owner-occupiers from not having to pay taxes on housing benefits exceeds the loss from being unable to deduct depreciation

  • As a result, the tax code involves a subsidy to homeownership

    • Treating an owner-occupier as a landlord renting to herself would eliminate this tax subsidy

  • Why is homeownership subsidized?

Costs of Housing - Owner-Occupied


  • To incorporate the tax-deductibility of mortgage interest and property taxes, one needs to adjust the previous formula for the owner-occupier's housing cost

    • For every dollar of mortgage interest the owner-occupier pays, she can reduce her taxes by dollars through the tax deduction. The same is true for property taxes
    • Later, we assume that rises with income

  • Hence, the cost per unit of housing for the owner-occupier is given by:

Costs of Housing - Rental

  • Consider a landlord who purchases a dwelling at a per-unit price vv and rents it out at per-unit price pp.

    • The competition in the housing market drives profit down to zero (same as in the house developers case), and pp is determined by the landlord's after-tax income minus after-tax costs

  • Just as the owner-occupier, the landlord buys the property with a 100% mortgage. Also, assume his income-tax rate equal to . Besides the mortgage interest rates ii, the proprietor also incurs in depreciation dd and property taxes hh. Tenure Choice

  • In the owner-occupier's cost expression, there is the income-tax rate , which differs across consumers: is higher for high-income and low for low-income residents. On the other hand, the renter's cost does not depend on the income-tax rate

  • When is small (low-income household), the cost of owning a house is higher than the cost of renting. However, after a certain ^^ (high-income household), owning becomes cheaper than renting

  • The landlord will have a high tax rate ()() since is likely to be a rich individual

Tenure Choice


  • This model can generate additional predictions. Consider and increase in the landlord's income-tax rate (higher top tax rate). Since a larger decreases the renter user-cost, that will shift the horizontal line downward

  • As a result, the cutoff tax rate ^^ rises, decreasing the number of households that are homeowners

  • Intuitively, the higher tax rate makes the excess depreciation deduction worth more to landlords, and this larger benefit is passed on to consumers in lower rents. The result is a decline in the homeownership rate

Other Factors

Other factors not considered in the model may also affect tenure decision:

  • Expected mobility

  • Pride of ownership

  • Risk

    • "Company towns": bad economic times translate into decreasing income and real estate prices

  • Home-maintenance tasks

  • Credit market & Down-payment requirements

    • Usually, a home buyer is required to make a substancial down payment
    • Low-income households may face credit constraints/higher mortgage interest rates

Housing Bubbles and Inflation

  • Although the capital-gains effect provides the key to understanding house bubbles, let us consider the effect of inflation

  • Suppose that the rate of overall inflation rises by 1%. Since nominal interest rates usually follow real interest rates + inflation: i=r+i=r+, the mortgage interest rate also rises by 1%. Also, assume g goes up by 1%

  • General inflation reduces the user cost and encourages owner-occupiers to demand more housing
