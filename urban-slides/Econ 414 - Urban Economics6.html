Detroit before cars

  • Detroit had ready access to wood, iron, and the Great Lakes system. Many boats were floating by that area, and it was natural for the city to specialize in ship engines. That expertise would make Detroit a natural place to build cars

  • That environment attracted innovators in the automobile sector

  • At the end of the nineteenth century, Detroit looked like the Silicon Valley in the 1970s

  • However, based on the knowledge-destroying idea

    • Not all technologies increase the returns from knowledge
    • Mass production took the place of innovation

  • After all, Detroit was no more the place where small and dynamic firms compete.

The industrial city

"What is good for General Motors is good for the country and vice-versa"

  • New machines and new management practices raised manufacturing industries' productivity, pushed prices down, and wages up. Consumption exploded.

  • The stability of a good, well-paid job in a factory was the American Dream

  • In 1950, Detroit was the third most prosperous city in the U.S.

  • Between 1950 and 2008, Detroit lost over a million people

  • Good manufacturing jobs helped the U.S. transition from a low-income to a middle-class society. Now, manufacturing is no longer the engine of prosperity for local communities. What happened?

Globalization & Technological Progress


  • Easy to outsource the production of certain goods

  • Globalization has an uneven effect: less-skilled workers tend to lose their jobs. On the other hand, the products are cheaper. Therefore, the "China shock" does not have the same impact everywhere

  • Less manufacturing jobs affect the whole community: for each manufacturing job lost, 1.6 jobs are eventually lost outside that sector

  • Some firms react to this process increasing productivity - they increase R & D investment, produce more patents, etc.

  • Increase in productivity means companies need fewer workers. However, it also means lower prices and higher wages

    • At the same time, technologies tend to favor highly skilled workers (engineers vs. blue-collar workers in manufacturing)

The Productivity Paradox

  • Although it is a fact that U.S. economic activity has steadily shifted away from goods and toward services, until 2010, the United States had the world's largest manufacturing sector. Besides, the U.S. manufacturing output keeps expanding over time

  • Over the last half-century, there are two contradictory features displayed by the trends of shares of GDP and employment by sector:

  1. The number of manufacturing jobs in the U.S. dropped from over 17 million in 2000 - 20.41% of total employment in the economy - to just over 12 million in 2015 (less than 15%)
  2. The manufacturing share of real GDP is constant over time
  • If production keeps increasing, how come manufacturing jobs are decreasing? If jobs are not in manufacturing, where are they?

Source: Baily and Bosworth, JEP 2014.

Innovation jobs


  • U.S. economy has gradually shifted away from traditional manufacturing toward the creation of knowledge, ideas, innovation

  • Innovation jobs are not easily defined because innovation takes many forms

  • But only 10% of the jobs in the U.S. belong to the innovation sector. How can that be the economic engine?

  • The only way to raise people's standard of living is to increase their productivity. Productivity is growing faster in the high-tech sector, and that means higher salaries for high-tech workers and workers in other sectors

  • In the long run, each new high-tech job in a metropolitan area is linked to five additional jobs in other sectors

  • What's so special about high-tech companies?

Innovation jobs


  • The primary production input in scientific research is human capital. That is one of the key reasons the innovation sector remains labor-intensive and has so many jobs

  • The rise of the innovation sector is associated with an increase in the value of talent; the economic returns on new ideas have never been so high

  • That is due to globalization and technological progress

    • Global markets enable the company to sell more without increasing production costs
    • Outsourcing parts of the innovation process to cheaper locations allows the company to spend more in R&D

  • Who benefits from the economic value created by innovations?

The great divergence


  • Gaps between more and less educated areas have become quite large - larger than would be predicted solely by the general rise in the returns to skills

    • Skills have increasingly come to dictate metropolitan fortunes

  • Unemployment rates during the recession are also correlated with the level of education in the area

  • Metropolitan areas with fewer adults with a college degree (as % of total) in 1940 experienced smaller increases in that share over the next sixty years. The opposite happened with metro areas with a higher percentage of adults with a college degree in the past

  • The increasing selection of skilled people into high-skill agglomerations seems likely to continue

  • What about the cost of living in those areas?

    • Why does Walmart like San Francisco?

  • Although the desire to reduce transportation costs for goods has become less important, the desire to reduce transportation costs for people and ideas is more critical

    • For certain types of production, geography is becoming more, nor less, important
